Florida Red Tide Events 2007-2015

This map displays significant red tide events, or harmful algae blooms (HAB) caused by the bacteria Karenia brevis in Florida from the years 2007-2015 provided from the Florida Fish & Wildlife Commission (FWC). Hovering over every circle (red tide event) provides a popup with specific information about that red tide event and sliding the bar in the bottom left toggles the map between the years 2007-2015. A significant red tide event is distinguished by a water sample that has a concentration of 10,000 or Karenia brevis cells/liter, which can have adverse affects on marine and human health.

What is Karenia brevis?

Karenia brevis is a single-cell algae responsible for causing "Florida red tide" events, which is a unique moniker that distinguishes red tide events caused by K. brevis from other types of algae. Typically occuring in late summer and early autumn, red tide blooms develop offshore and can be brought inshore by currents and winds. There is no direct link between human activity and blooms, although once blooms move inshore, human generated pollution sources can proliferate existing blooms.

<1,000,000 High As above plus water discoloration

Red Tide Effects on Marine and Human Health and Tourism

K. brevis produce powerful neurotoxins called brevetoxins that that can adversely affect fish, seabirds, turtles, and marine mammals and affect human health by causing respiratory irritation or shellfish poisoning if shellfish contaminated by the nueortoxins are consumed. Less frequently, K. brevis red tide blooms can cause fish kills by depleting the water of dissolved oxygen and brevotoxins paralyzing the central nervous systems of fish. K. brevis red tide blooms can even be fatal to dolphins and manatees.

Various shellfish like clams, mussels, and oysters can accumulate brevetoxins and if consumed by humans can cause Neurotoxic Shellfish Poisoning (NSP). FWC describes any red tide events with a K. brevis concentration of higher than 10,000 cells/liter as a harmful algal bloom event that can cause fish kills. Low concentrations of K. brevis are defined as 10,000 to 100,000 cells/liter. Medium concentration of K. brevis, 100,000 to 1,000,000 cells/liter can cause respiratory irritation in humans and are likely to cause fish kills. High concentrations of K. brevis, greater than 1,000,000 cells/liter have a high probability of causing fish kills.

If any of these events occur close to shore they can have a severe adverse affect on tourism as water at beaches become discolored, foul smelling, and can cause illnesses to humans.

Data Collection Methods

These samples were collected by the FWC and volunteers over this timespan and do not comprehensively represent every single significant Florida red tide event occurred over this timespan. Many samples are also collected in response to blooms. More information on FWC's datasets can be found here.
